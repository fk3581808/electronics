1. Product Life Cycle (PLC)

PYQ Focus

Define Product Life Cycle. Explain the different stages involved. Discuss the concept of PLC including its stages for marketing strategy. Explain factors: Product Cost, Sale, Profit and Marketing strategy.

A. What is the Product Life Cycle (PLC)?

The Product Life Cycle is the course that a product's sales and profits take over its lifetime. It traces the journey of a product from its initial introduction into the market until it is eventually removed or replaced.

Easy Word Explanation

Just like a human being is born, grows into a teenager, reaches mature adulthood, and eventually grows old and dies, a product goes through the exact same journey in the market.

B. Stages of the Product Life Cycle Crucial 6-Mark PYQ

A standard PLC has four distinct stages. In exams, explain how Sales, Profits, Costs, and Marketing Strategies change in each stage.

1. Introduction Stage (The "Birth")

The product is launched into the market for the very first time.

  • Sales: Very low, because people don't know about it yet.
  • Cost: Extremely high per customer (due to heavy R&D and manufacturing setup costs).
  • Profit: Negative (The company is losing money at this stage).
  • Marketing Strategy: Create heavy product awareness. Spend massive amounts of money on advertising to educate early adopters.
Electronics Ex: When Apple launched the very first Apple Vision Pro.

2. Growth Stage (The "Teenage Years")

If the new product satisfies the market, sales start climbing quickly as more people buy it.

  • Sales: Rapidly rising.
  • Cost: Cost per customer drops as the factory produces more units (Economies of scale).
  • Profit: Profits start rising and hit their peak towards the end of this stage.
  • Marketing Strategy: Maximize market share. Shift advertising from "awareness" to "building brand preference." Competitors start entering the market.
Electronics Ex: Smartwatches and True Wireless Earbuds (TWS) a few years ago.

3. Maturity Stage (The "Adulthood")

The product is established. Most potential buyers already own it. Sales growth slows down and levels off.

  • Sales: Peak sales (highest volume), but growth has stopped.
  • Cost: Lowest cost per customer. Production is highly efficient.
  • Profit: High profits, but they start dropping because you have to lower prices to fight competitors.
  • Marketing Strategy: Defend market share. Add new features, change packaging, and offer massive discounts or warranties to steal customers from rival brands.
Electronics Ex: Smartphones today (iPhone 15). Companies add slightly better cameras to keep selling.

4. Decline Stage (The "Old Age")

Sales drop drastically because of new technological advances or changes in customer tastes.

  • Sales: Rapidly falling.
  • Cost: Low cost, but inefficient due to low volume.
  • Profit: Drops to zero or becomes negative again.
  • Marketing Strategy: Reduce spending entirely. "Harvest" the product by milking the last few loyal customers, or phase it out completely.
Electronics Ex: DVD Players, wired earphones, and physical calculators.

2. Customer Need Identification

PYQ Focus

Explain customer need identification in product design. Importance. How can organizations effectively identify customer needs. Describe the process/role of market research.

A. What is Customer Need Identification?

It is the process of discovering, understanding, and documenting exactly what the customer requires from a product before the engineering team starts designing it.

Easy Word Explanation

It is asking the user "What is your actual problem?" instead of just guessing. If you guess wrong, you will spend millions designing a perfect product that nobody wants to buy.

B. Importance of Customer Need Identification PYQ

Prevents Market Failure

Ensures the product solves a real problem. (e.g., Making a highly complex TV remote when older people just want large, simple buttons).

Guides Engineering

Gives clear targets. (e.g., The customer wants a phone that lasts 2 days → Engineering knows they must use a 5000mAh battery).

Hidden Needs (Latent)

Customers often don't know what they want until you ask the right questions. Identifying these creates breakthrough products.

C. The Process: How to Effectively Identify Needs PYQ

To properly identify needs, organizations use a 4-step process relying heavily on Market Research:

1. Gather Raw Data from Customers:
  • Interviews: Sitting 1-on-1 with a customer and asking them how they use a product.
  • Focus Groups: Putting 10 people in a room and letting them discuss their frustrations with current products.
  • Observation: Just watching a customer use a product silently. (You might notice a mechanic struggling to hold a flashlight while soldering—Boom! A need for a soldering iron with a built-in LED light is identified).
2. Interpret Raw Data into "Needs":

Translate the customer's exact words into an engineering statement.
(Customer says: "This laptop gets too hot on my lap." → Need: "The laptop must dissipate heat away from the bottom").

3. Organize the Needs into a Hierarchy:

Group similar needs together into Primary (Main) and Secondary (Detailed) needs.

4. Establish Relative Importance:

Ask customers to rate which needs are absolute "Must-Haves" vs "Nice-to-Haves."

3. The Challenges of Product Development

PYQ Focus

Identify and explain three major challenges commonly encountered in product development in detail.

Developing a new product is incredibly difficult. An engineer faces three major challenges:

1. Trade-offs (The Balancing Act)

Explanation: A trade-off is when you must sacrifice one good feature to gain another. An engineer cannot have everything perfectly.

Electronics Example: You want to design an ultra-thin, lightweight smartphone. But you also want a battery that lasts 3 days. The Challenge: A massive battery is heavy and thick. You cannot have both. You must make a painful trade-off and find a compromise.

2. Time Pressure (The Race)

Explanation: Technology changes at lightning speed. A complex electronic product can take 2 to 3 years to design and manufacture.

The Challenge: By the time you finish building your cutting-edge product and launch it, a competitor might have released something better, or the technology might already be outdated. Teams must work under immense stress to shrink development time without reducing quality.

3. Economics and Cost Limitations

Explanation: An engineer could design the greatest smartwatch in the world using titanium and diamond glass. But if it costs ₹5 Lakhs to manufacture, nobody will buy it.

The Challenge: The product must be designed not only to be high quality and feature-rich, but it must be cheap enough to produce in factories so the company can sell it at a reasonable price and still make a profit.

4. Characteristics of Successful Product Development

PYQ Focus

Discuss the key characteristics that contribute to a successful product development.

How does a company know if their product development project was a success? It is judged on 5 key characteristics:

1. Product Quality

Does it actually work? Is it reliable? Does it satisfy the customer's needs? A high-quality product leads to high market share.

2. Product Cost

What is the manufacturing cost? This determines how much profit the company makes on every unit sold.

3. Development Time

How fast did the team complete the project? Fast time means beating competitors to market and recovering investment quickly.

4. Development Cost

How much money did the company burn on R&D, salaries, and prototyping? This must be kept within budget.

5. Development Capability

Did the team learn new skills during this project? A successful project makes the team smarter, meaning their next product will be developed even faster and better.

5. Phases of a Generic Product Development Process

PYQ Focus

Describe the phases of a generic product development process in brief.

Creating a product from scratch follows a structured, step-by-step process. (This expands on the 7-step NPD process from Unit 1, focusing heavily on engineering and design).

0

Phase 0: Planning (Pre-Project)

Happens before the project is officially approved. Management decides the general market target (e.g., "Let's build a smart fitness ring").

1

Phase 1: Concept Development

Target market needs are identified. Alternative concepts are generated and evaluated, and one is selected. (e.g., Drawing sketches of the ring, deciding it tracks heart rate and sleep).

2

Phase 2: System-Level Design

The product is broken down into separate subsystems. General architecture is defined.

Electronics Ex: Deciding the ring will have 3 subsystems: (1) Bluetooth module, (2) Optical sensor board, and (3) Battery charging circuit.

3

Phase 3: Detail Design

The heavy engineering phase. Every single part is explicitly defined. CAD models are drawn. Exact resistors, capacitors, PCB layout finalized. Tolerances and materials chosen.

4

Phase 4: Testing and Refinement

Multiple prototypes are built.

  • Alpha Prototypes: Built with prototype parts just to see if electronics work.
  • Beta Prototypes: Built using factory machines and given to real customers to test for bugs.

Phase 5: Production Ramp-Up

The product is made using the intended production system. Purpose is to train the factory workforce and work out bugs in manufacturing machines before mass launch.

6. Product Analysis and Characteristics

PYQ Focus

Write any four key product characteristics that influence consumer choice. Explain the characteristics of product.

When a consumer stands in an electronics shop, why do they pick Product A over Product B? They analyze specific characteristics.

Four Key Product Characteristics Influencing Consumer Choice:

1. Aesthetics and Design (Form)

How the product looks, feels, and sounds. Apple products sell massively because of their premium metallic feel and clean design.

2. Functionality and Performance

Does it do what it is supposed to do efficiently? (e.g., A gamer chooses a laptop based purely on the high performance of its graphics card).

3. Reliability and Durability

Consumers want a product that will not break down after a month. A brand known for tough, long-lasting products (like a Casio G-Shock) heavily influences choice.

4. Usability and Ergonomics

How easy is it to use? If a smart-TV has a remote with 100 confusing buttons, consumers will avoid it. A simple, intuitive user interface wins.

7. Economic, Production, and Marketing Aspects

PYQ Focus

Explain the economic considerations that impact product development. Briefly explain the relationship between production and marketing aspects.

A. Economic Considerations in Product Design PYQ

Designing a product isn't just about engineering; it's about money.

  • Tooling Cost vs. Unit Cost: Making custom plastic injection molds (tooling) costs lakhs of rupees. If you only plan to sell 100 units, the tooling cost makes the product too expensive.
  • Return on Investment (ROI): The company must calculate: "If we spend ₹1 Crore developing this drone, will we make ₹5 Crores back in 2 years?"
  • Break-Even Point: Calculating exactly how many units must be sold before the company stops losing money and starts making a profit.

B. Relationship Between Production and Marketing PYQ

There is always a massive tug-of-war (relationship) between these two departments.

The Marketing Team

Wants to offer the customer 50 different colors, unlimited customized features, and the lowest possible price to boost sales.

The Production Team

Hates customization. They want to produce exactly one color with exactly one standard feature because it is cheaper, faster, and reduces factory errors.

The Relationship/Balance:

Successful product development requires a compromise. They might agree to use a modular design—the production team builds one standard electronic base, and the marketing team sells easily swappable colored outer cases to satisfy the customer.
