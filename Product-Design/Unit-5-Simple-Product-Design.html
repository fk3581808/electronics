1. Stages from Need till Manufacture

PYQ Focus

Describe the stages from need till the manufacture of the product. (6 Marks)

This topic acts as a grand summary of the entire Product Design subject. The journey of a product starts with a human problem and ends on a factory assembly line.

Easy Word Explanation

It is the complete journey of a product from being just a "thought" in an engineer's brain to becoming a physical "thing" packed in a box, ready to be sold.

The 7 Stages of End-to-End Product Development 6-Mark PYQ

1

1.Identification of Need (The Trigger)

Every product starts because a customer has an unfulfilled need or a problem. The company conducts market research, surveys, and focus groups to identify this exact gap in the market.

Example: People complain that their phone batteries die during long train journeys, and there are no plug points. The Need is portable power.

2

2.Conceptualization & Idea Generation

The engineering and design teams brainstorm hundreds of possible solutions to solve the need. They use techniques like morphological analysis to generate rough concepts.

Example: Should we make a solar-powered hat? A hand-crank charger? Or a lithium-ion Power Bank? The Power Bank concept is chosen.

3

3.Feasibility Study

The chosen concept is tested for technical and financial viability. Can we actually build this with today's electronics? Will it cost less than ₹1000 to manufacture so we can sell it for a profit? If yes, the project proceeds.

4

4.Detailed Design & Material Selection

This is the heavy engineering phase. For an electronic product, this involves:

  • Designing the schematic and PCB layout using software like Altium/KiCad.
  • Designing the outer 3D casing using CAD software (SolidWorks/AutoCAD).
  • Selecting exact components (e.g., a 10,000mAh Li-Po battery, a specific charging IC).
5

5.Prototyping and Testing

The digital CAD files are brought to life using Rapid Prototyping (3D printing). The PCB is chemically etched and soldered by hand. This physical model (Prototype) is tested to see if it overheats, if it charges properly, and if the case fits together. Fatal errors are fixed here.

6

6.Design for Manufacturing (DFM) & Tooling

The design is adjusted so that factory machines can build it quickly and cheaply. Custom steel molds are ordered for plastic injection molding. The SMT (Surface Mount Technology) pick-and-place machines are programmed to drop microchips onto the PCBs.

7. Final Manufacturing & Assembly

Mass production begins. Plastic casings are molded by the thousands. PCBs are baked in reflow ovens. Workers on an assembly line snap the battery and PCB into the plastic casing, screw it shut, test it, and put it in the final cardboard packaging.

2. Impact of Material Selection

PYQ Focus

Discuss the impact of material selection in product design. (6 Marks)

Material selection is one of the most critical decisions an engineer makes. You cannot build a submarine out of cardboard, and you wouldn't build a disposable spoon out of gold.

Easy Word Explanation (The Recipe Analogy)

Material selection is choosing the exact "ingredients" for your recipe. If you use cheap ingredients, the food tastes bad. If you use the wrong materials, the product breaks, melts, or becomes too expensive to sell.

The Impact of Material Selection on a Product: 6-Mark PYQ

Performance & Durability

The material dictates how strong the product is.

Electronics Example: A laptop casing made of Aluminum (like a MacBook) can survive drops and dissipates heat excellently. A laptop made of cheap ABS plastic might crack if dropped.

Cost & Profitability

Exotic materials eat into the company's profit. Titanium is incredibly strong and light, but it costs 100 times more than plastic.

Impact: To sell a budget smartphone for ₹8,000, the engineer must select polycarbonate (plastic) for the back cover. If they choose aerospace aluminum, the phone would cost ₹30,000, and the budget customer won't buy it.

Weight & Portability

For portable electronics (like drones, smartwatches, or wireless earphones), weight is critical. Engineers select materials like Carbon Fiber or Magnesium alloys because they offer maximum strength at minimum weight.

Aesthetics & "Feel"

Materials trigger psychological reactions in customers. Cold metal and glass feel "premium" and "expensive." Hollow, glossy plastic feels "cheap." Apple uses stainless steel and glass to justify charging ₹1 Lakh for a phone.

Thermal & Electrical Props

In electronics, material selection directly affects safety. The main circuit board must be made of FR4 (a flame-retardant fiberglass) so it doesn't catch fire if a short circuit happens. Heat sinks must be made of Copper or Aluminum because they are excellent thermal conductors.

Environment & Sustainability

Modern design requires eco-friendly materials. Choosing biodegradable plastics or ocean-bound recycled plastics reduces the product's environmental footprint (Green Design).

3. Role of Ergonomics in Product Design

PYQ Focus

Discuss the role of ergonomics in product design. (6 Marks)

A. What is Ergonomics?

Ergonomics (also known as Human Factors Engineering) is the applied science of designing and arranging things so that the people who use them interact with them safely, comfortably, and efficiently.

Easy Explanation: Ergonomics is designing the product to fit the human body, rather than forcing the human body to twist and hurt itself to fit the product.

B. Role and Importance of Ergonomics 6-Mark PYQ

1. Maximizing User Comfort (Physical Ergonomics)

A product must not cause physical pain or fatigue during prolonged use.

Electronics Example: A standard computer mouse forces your wrist to twist uncomfortably. An Ergonomic Mouse is sculpted vertically to match the natural resting position of the human hand, preventing Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (wrist pain).

2. Enhancing Usability & Efficiency (Cognitive)

It ensures the product is easy to understand without a manual.

Electronics Example: On a smartphone screen, the most frequently used buttons (like the spacebar or 'send' button) are placed at the bottom center—the exact ergonomic "thumb-reach zone" where a human thumb naturally rests.

3. Ensuring User Safety

Poor ergonomics can lead to fatal accidents. Emergency stop buttons on heavy factory machines are always made massive, bright red, and placed exactly at waist height so a worker can slam it instantly in a panic without looking.

4. Reducing Human Error

In complex electronics, like an airplane cockpit or a medical ventilator, ergonomic design ensures that switches are shaped differently or spaced far apart so a doctor or pilot doesn't accidentally flip the wrong switch in a stressful situation.

5. Market Competitiveness

If two companies make a gaming controller with the exact same electronics inside, the customer will always buy the one that has rubberized grips and curved triggers that "hug" the fingers perfectly (Ergonomics).

4. Case Study: Design of a Simple Product

Syllabus Focus

Design of simple products dealing with various aspects of product development.

To understand the syllabus practically, let us trace the end-to-end design of a simple electronic product: A Smart Soldering Iron for Diploma Students.

Step 1: Identifying the Need

The Problem: Diploma students frequently burn their PCBs because traditional cheap soldering irons get too hot, and professional soldering stations are too big to carry in a college bag.

The Need: A portable, pen-sized soldering iron with exact temperature control.

Step 2: Idea Generation & Conceptualization

The design team decides the iron should be powered by a standard USB Type-C laptop charger, have a tiny OLED screen to show temperature, and two buttons (+ and -) to adjust the heat.

Step 3: Material Selection & Ergonomics
  • Ergonomics: The body must be shaped like a thick pen with a rubberized grip near the tip so students can hold it steady for precise SMD (Surface Mount Device) soldering without hand fatigue.
  • Materials: The tip must be Copper (excellent heat transfer). The grip must be high-temperature Silicone (so fingers don't burn). The outer casing should be Aluminum (lightweight and durable).
Step 4: Detailed Design

Electronics engineers design a tiny PCB using a microcontroller and a PID control algorithm to precisely regulate the heat. Mechanical engineers design the aluminum shell in CAD.

Step 5: Prototyping

A 3D printer creates a plastic mock-up of the body to test if it feels comfortable in the hand. The PCB is printed and tested.

Iteration: During testing, they realize the buttons are too close to the hot tip. They redesign the PCB to move the buttons higher up.

Step 6: Manufacture

The final design is sent to the factory. The aluminum bodies are CNC machined. The PCBs are assembled by pick-and-place machines. The product is boxed and shipped to electronics stores for students to buy.
