1. Introduction to Data Communication

PYQ Focus (6 Marks)

Draw the block diagram of data communication system and state the function of each block. Describe the computer network used in data communication.

A. What is Data Communication?

Data Communication is formally defined as the exchange of data (in the form of digital 1s and 0s or analog signals) between two or more devices via some form of transmission medium (like a copper wire, optic cable, or wireless electromagnetic waves). For effective data communication to occur, the communicating devices must be part of a communication system made up of a combination of hardware (physical equipment) and software (programs).

Easy Word Explanation

Imagine you are talking to your friend on a phone call. Your voice is the 'data', the phone is the 'device', and the mobile network is the 'medium'. Data communication is just machines talking to each other instead of humans.

B. Block Diagram of a Data Communication System 6-Mark Breakdown

For a 6-mark question, always draw the block diagram first, followed by a detailed paragraph for each of the 5 components.

[ SENDER ] ---> [ MESSAGE + PROTOCOL ] ---> [ TRANSMISSION MEDIUM ] ---> [ RECEIVER ]

Message

  • The message is the actual information or data to be communicated.
  • It can consist of text, numbers, pictures, audio, or video, or any combination of these.
  • In modern digital communication, this message is broken down into binary bits (0s and 1s) before transmission.

Sender (Transmitter)

  • The sender is the physical device that generates the message and sends the data over the network.
  • It comprises the hardware and software needed to encode the message into signals.
  • Electronics Example: A computer workstation, a smartphone, a CCTV camera, or an IoT temperature sensor.

Receiver

  • The receiver is the device that accepts the incoming signal, decodes it, and retrieves the original message sent by the sender.
  • The receiver must be capable of processing the specific type of signal generated by the sender.
  • Electronics Example: A web server, a smart TV receiving a Netflix stream, or a printer receiving a print command.

Transmission Medium

This is the physical or logical path by which a message travels from the sender to the receiver. It provides the "highway" for the data signals.

  • Guided Media (Wired): Twisted pair copper cable, Coaxial cable, or Fiber Optic cable.
  • Unguided Media (Wireless): Radio waves, Microwaves, or Infrared (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth).

Protocol

  • A protocol is a formal set of rules, conventions, and data structures that govern data communication.
  • It represents an agreement between the communicating devices. Without a protocol, two devices may be physically connected but cannot understand each other's data (just like a person speaking only Japanese cannot communicate with a person speaking only French).
  • Example: TCP (Transmission Control Protocol), IP (Internet Protocol), HTTP.

2. Concept of Analog and Digital Signals & Bandwidth

PYQ Focus (6 Marks)

Differentiate between Analog and Digital signal using any four parameters. Explain with suitable waveforms.

Data (like voice, text, or video) must be converted into electromagnetic signals to travel across a network. These signals are strictly categorized into two types: Analog and Digital.

A. Analog Signals

An Analog Signal is a continuous, time-varying electromagnetic wave that changes smoothly over a given period. It can take on an infinite number of values within a specific continuous range.

  • Waveform Characteristics: It is typically represented by a Sine Wave. It has key characteristics like Amplitude (peak height), Frequency (number of cycles per second), and Phase (position of the wave at time zero).
  • Electronics Context: Older electronic systems, such as AM/FM radio broadcasting, traditional landline telephones, and CRT televisions, relied entirely on analog signals.
Easy Ex: Think of an analog clock with continuously moving sweep hands, or a fan regulator where you can smoothly increase the speed to any micro-level between 0 and 5.

B. Digital Signals

A Digital Signal is a discrete, non-continuous signal that carries information in binary form (0s and 1s). It jumps suddenly and instantly between two distinct voltage levels (e.g., +5V for logic '1' and 0V for logic '0').

  • Waveform Characteristics: It is represented by a Square Wave (sharp, flat-topped steps with sudden vertical drops/rises).
  • Electronics Context: Modern computers, microprocessors, fiber optic communications, and digital ICs process only digital signals because they are highly resistant to noise and easier to store.
Easy Ex: Think of a simple light switch. It is either completely ON (1) or completely OFF (0). There is no "in-between."

C. Differences Between Analog and Digital Signals 6-Mark PYQ Table

Parameter Analog Signal Digital Signal
1. Nature of Signal Continuous over time. Values change smoothly. Discrete over time. Jumps strictly between 0 and 1.
2. Waveform Shape Represented by a continuous Sine Wave. Represented by a step-like Square Wave.
3. Susceptibility to Noise Highly affected by noise. During transmission, external electromagnetic noise mixes with the wave, degrading quality severely. Less affected by noise. Even if slight noise is added, the hardware can easily distinguish between a high (+5V) and low (0V) state.
4. Bandwidth Requirement Requires relatively less bandwidth for transmission. Requires higher bandwidth because sudden square-wave transitions contain many high-frequency components.
5. Storage and Processing Difficult to store data and requires complex analog circuitry (Op-Amps) to process. Extremely easy to store (on Hard Drives, SSDs, RAM) and easy to process using logic gates.
6. Examples Human voice, Thermocouple output, FM Radio. Data in PCs, HDMI video, Digital watches.

D. What is Bandwidth?

Bandwidth is defined as the difference between the highest and lowest frequencies contained in a signal. In the context of computer networking, bandwidth refers to the maximum data transfer rate of a network or internet connection. It measures how much data can be sent over a specific connection in a given amount of time.

  • Unit of Measurement: Bits Per Second (bps), Megabits per second (Mbps), or Gigabits per second (Gbps).

Easy Word Explanation (The Water Pipe)

Think of bandwidth as a water pipe. A wider pipe (high bandwidth) allows more water (data) to flow through simultaneously than a narrow pipe (low bandwidth). A 100 Mbps fiber connection will download a 1GB movie much faster than a 10 Mbps copper connection.

3. Computer Networks and Their Types

PYQ Focus (6 Marks)

Define the computer network and brief different types. Explain Personal Area Networks and state two advantages and disadvantages.

A. What is a Computer Network?

A Computer Network is a system of interconnected computers, peripheral devices (like printers, scanners), and networking hardware (like routers, switches) that are linked together to facilitate the sharing of data, hardware resources, and software applications. The connection can be established using physical wires or wireless radio waves.

B. Types of Computer Networks (Based on Geographical Span)

To score full marks, provide a definition, range, and pros/cons for the network types.

PYQ Focus

1. PAN (Personal Area Network)

  • Definition: A network organized around an individual person within a very short range, used for connecting personal devices.
  • Range: Typically within 10 meters (30 feet).
  • Technologies used: Bluetooth, ZigBee, Infrared, USB.
Advantages:
(1) Highly secure because of the extremely short range (hard for hackers to intercept from far away).
(2) Very low cost, low power consumption, and no complex wiring required.
Disadvantages:
(1) Extremely limited distance/coverage (only works in the same room).
(2) Very slow data transfer speeds compared to standard LANs.

2. LAN (Local Area Network)

  • Definition: A private network designed to operate over a small, localized physical area, such as a single office building, a school campus, or a home.
  • Characteristics: High data transfer rates (100 Mbps to 10 Gbps), privately owned by a single organization, and low error rates.
  • Technology used: Ethernet cables (Cat 6), Wi-Fi (WLAN), Network Switches.
  • Example: The computer lab in your diploma college where 50 computers are connected to one main server using Ethernet cables.

3. MAN (Metropolitan Area Network)

  • Definition: A network that covers a larger geographic area than a LAN, typically spanning an entire city or a large university campus. It essentially connects multiple LANs together.
  • Characteristics: Owned by a consortium of users or a single network provider who sells the service. Uses high-capacity backbone technologies like fiber optics.
  • Example: Your local Cable TV network spread across your city, or a city-wide municipal public Wi-Fi network.

4. WAN (Wide Area Network)

  • Definition: A vast telecommunications network that covers a large geographical area, such as a state, a country, or the entire globe.
  • Characteristics: Data speeds are generally slower than LANs due to massive distances. It heavily relies on public telecommunication infrastructure and leased lines. High error rates compared to LAN.
  • Technology used: Satellites, deep-sea submarine optic cables, microwave towers.
  • Example: The ATM network of the State Bank of India (all ATMs across India connected to Mumbai servers), or 4G/5G cellular mobile networks.

5. Internetwork (The Internet)

Definition: An "Internetwork" is formed when two or more distinct networks (like multiple LANs and WANs) are connected together using routers. The ultimate and largest example of an internetwork is the Internet itself, which connects billions of devices globally.

4. Computer Network Topologies

PYQ Focus (6 Marks each)

Describe different types in brief. Explain Mesh and Star topology. Explain Tree topology for a 3-storey building.

Topology refers to the physical geometric layout or the logical arrangement of a network. It defines how the nodes (computers/devices) are wired together and how data flows between them.

1. Bus Topology

Description: All network devices are connected to a single, continuous main cable (called the backbone or bus) with terminators at both ends. Data sent by one node travels along the bus until it reaches the target node.

Advantages: Very cheap, requires the least amount of cable, and is easy to set up for small networks.

Disadvantages: If the main central cable breaks or is cut, the entire network crashes. Performance degrades drastically as more computers are added (due to data collisions).

Highly Important for Exams

2. Star Topology

Description: Every single device on the network has a dedicated point-to-point physical connection to a central controller device (usually a Hub or a Network Switch). Devices cannot talk to each other directly; data must flow through the central switch.

Advantages:

  • Highly robust: If one cable connecting a computer breaks, only that specific computer is disconnected. The rest of the network continues to work perfectly.
  • Easy to troubleshoot and identify faults.
  • Easy to add or remove devices without disrupting the network.

Disadvantages: If the central Hub/Switch fails, the entire network goes dead immediately. It requires more cable length than a Bus topology.

3. Ring Topology

Description: Each device is connected to exactly two neighboring devices, forming a closed loop or physical circle. Data travels continuously in one direction (unidirectional) from node to node until it reaches its destination.

Advantages: Orderly network where every device gets equal access to transmit data (using token passing).

Disadvantages: Highly vulnerable. If one computer crashes or one link breaks, the data chain is broken, and the entire network stops functioning.

Highly Important for Exams

4. Mesh Topology

Description: A highly complex layout where every single device is connected to every other device in the network via dedicated point-to-point links. (Formula for links: n(n-1)/2).

Advantages:
  • Ultimate Reliability/Fault Tolerance: If one cable breaks, there are multiple alternative routing paths. The network almost never goes down.
  • High Security & Privacy: Dedicated links mean data goes only to intended recipient.
Disadvantages:

Extremely expensive and practically impossible to wire for large networks because it requires an massive amount of cabling and I/O ports.

5. Tree Topology 6-Mark PYQ: 3-Storey Building

Description: It is a hierarchical hybrid topology that combines characteristics of Star and Bus topologies. It has a root node (main server/router), and all other nodes are connected in a branching hierarchy.

6-Mark Exam Answer for 3-Storey Building:

To network a 3-storey office, a Tree Topology is the most efficient and practical choice.

  • Ground Floor
    (Basement/Server Room): The main Server and the Main Building Router (The Root Node) are installed here. A high-speed vertical backbone cable (the "trunk" of the tree) runs from this router up through the building.
  • 1st Floor
    A local Network Switch is placed here, connected directly to the vertical backbone. All 20 computers on the 1st floor are connected to this switch in a Star arrangement.
  • 2nd Floor
    Another Network Switch is placed here, connected to the backbone. Computers on this floor connect to this local switch.
  • 3rd Floor
    A third Network Switch is placed here, connected to the backbone.
Justification:

This structure looks like an inverted tree. It is highly fault-tolerant. If the 2nd-floor switch fails or power trips, only the 2nd floor loses its network. The 1st and 3rd floors will continue to work perfectly and can still communicate with the ground floor server.

6. Daisy Chain Topology

Description: Devices are connected sequentially, one right after the other in a linear line (A connects to B, B connects to C, C connects to D). Unlike a ring, the two ends are not connected back to each other.

7. Hybrid Topology

Description: A combination of two or more different standard topologies to form a larger, complex network (e.g., combining a Star topology in the HR department and a Ring topology in the Finance department, connected by a Bus backbone).

5. Network Architecture Models: OSI and TCP/IP

PYQ Focus (6 Marks)

Compare OSI and TCP/IP. List the 7 layers of OSI and explain the function of each layer in brief. Sketch layered architecture.

To allow computers manufactured by different companies (like Apple, IBM, Microsoft) to communicate globally, standard networking models were created to govern how data is transmitted.

A. The OSI Reference Model (Open Systems Interconnection)

Developed by ISO (International Organization for Standardization) in 1984, it is a theoretical, 7-layer framework that standardizes the functions of a telecommunication system.

Easy Word Explanation (The Corporate Parcel)

Sending data is like sending a physical corporate parcel. The parcel goes from the Boss (App Layer) -> Translator (Presentation) -> Dispatch Manager (Session) -> Packaging Dept (Transport) -> Sorting Facility (Network) -> Local Delivery Van (Data Link) -> The physical road (Physical Layer). Each layer does exactly one specific job and passes it down.

The 7 Layers of OSI (Bottom to Top Explanation for Full Marks)

Mnemonic: Please Do Not Throw Sausage Pizza Away
7
Application Layer (Top Layer)

Function: The topmost layer. It provides the actual user interface and network services to the end-user's applications. It is the window through which users and application processes access network services.

Examples: Web browsing (HTTP), Email (SMTP), File Transfer (FTP).

6
Presentation Layer

Function: It prepares the data for the Application layer. It acts as a data translator.

  • Translation: Converts data from computer-specific formats into standard network formats (like ASCII).
  • Encryption/Decryption: Secures the data for privacy.
  • Compression: Reduces bits to save bandwidth (e.g., compressing raw video into MP4).
5
Session Layer

Function: It acts as the network dialog controller. It establishes, maintains, synchronizes, and ultimately terminates the communication session between two computers.

Key roles: Adding synchronization checkpoints (so if a 1GB download fails at 900MB, it resumes from 900MB, not 0MB).

4
Transport Layer

Function: It is the heart of the OSI model. It ensures complete end-to-end delivery of the entire message.

  • Segmentation: Divides large messages into smaller units called "Segments".
  • Connection Control: Establishes a logical connection (TCP for reliable, UDP for fast).
  • Flow Control: Ensures a fast sender doesn't overwhelm a slow receiver.
3
Network Layer

Function: Responsible for source-to-destination delivery of packets across multiple different networks.

  • Logical Addressing: Assigns unique IP addresses to sender and receiver.
  • Routing: Determines the best, shortest path for data to travel. (Routers work here).
2
Data Link Layer

Function: Ensures node-to-node, error-free transfer of data over the physical layer.

  • Framing: Breaks packets from Network layer into manageable units called "Frames".
  • Physical Addressing: Adds sender/receiver MAC addresses to the frame.
  • Error Control: Detects and retransmits damaged frames. (Switches work here).
1
Physical Layer (Bottom Layer)

Function: Deals with the mechanical and electrical specifications of the network. It receives raw data bits (0s and 1s) from the Data Link layer and converts them into physical signals (electrical voltage, light pulses, or radio waves) to be transmitted over the physical medium.

Key roles: Bit synchronization, defining the physical topology, and controlling the bit rate.

B. TCP/IP Reference Model

The TCP/IP model is the practical, real-world protocol suite that the modern Internet actually runs on. Developed by the US Department of Defense (DARPA), it condensed the theoretical 7 OSI layers into just 4 functional layers:

1. Network Access Layer (Link Layer)

Combines the functions of OSI's Physical and Data Link layers. It handles hardware addressing and physical transmission.

2. Internet Layer

Equivalent to OSI's Network layer. It handles IP addressing and routing packets across the internet.

3. Transport Layer

Equivalent to OSI's Transport layer. It handles end-to-end communication using TCP (reliable) or UDP (fast).

4. Application Layer

Combines OSI's Session, Presentation, and Application layers into one massive layer that handles all software protocols (HTTP, FTP, DNS).

C. Comparison: OSI vs TCP/IP 6-Mark PYQ

Feature OSI Reference Model TCP/IP Reference Model
Full Form Open Systems Interconnection. Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol.
Number of Layers Consists of exactly 7 well-defined layers. Consists of only 4 broad layers.
Nature & Usage It is a theoretical, conceptual reference model. Rarely used in practice today. It is a highly practical, implementation-based model. The entire modern Internet is based on it.
Development Origin Developed by ISO (International Organization for Standardization). Developed by DARPA (US Department of Defense).
Layer Strictness Very strict boundaries between layers. Functions are clearly separated. Looser boundaries; the Application layer handles multiple complex jobs simultaneously.
Delivery Guarantee Network layer guarantees both connection-oriented and connectionless services. Internet layer only supports connectionless (IP) services. Delivery guarantee is pushed to the Transport layer (TCP).

6. Transmission Media: Wired and Wireless

PYQ Focus (6 Marks)

Explain various transmission media. Compare guided and unguided media. Analyse special features of wired vs wireless connectivity with examples.

Transmission media provides the physical path connecting the sender and receiver. It is broadly categorized into Guided (Wired) and Unguided (Wireless).

A. Guided Media (Wired / Bound)

In guided media, the signal energy is strictly contained and guided through a solid physical conduit (wire or cable).

1. Twisted Pair Cable

Construction: Consists of two insulated copper wires twisted around each other in a spiral pattern. Twisting is crucial because it cancels out Electromagnetic Interference (EMI) and "cross-talk" from adjacent wires.

Types: Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP - common LAN cables like Cat5e/Cat6) and Shielded Twisted Pair (STP - has extra metal foil for industrial environments).

Usage: Standard office LAN networks and old telephone lines.

2. Coaxial Cable

Construction: Has a solid central copper core (which carries data), surrounded by a plastic insulator, which is then wrapped in a braided metal wire mesh shield (to block massive outside interference), covered by an outer plastic jacket.

Usage: Used extensively for Cable TV networks and high-speed broadband internet lines.

3. Optical Fiber Cable (OFC)

Construction: Uses ultra-pure glass or plastic threads (the core) surrounded by a reflective cladding.

Working: It transmits data using pulses of light instead of electrical voltage, utilizing the principle of Total Internal Reflection.

Features: Offers incredibly high bandwidth (Gigabits to Terabits per second), is 100% immune to electrical interference, and can transmit data over hundreds of kilometers without losing signal strength.

B. Unguided Media (Wireless / Unbound)

In unguided media, data is transmitted through the air (or vacuum of space) using electromagnetic waves. No physical cabling is required.

Radio Waves

Features: Frequencies ranging from 3 kHz to 1 GHz. They are generally omnidirectional (travel in all directions from the antenna). They can easily penetrate through solid walls.

Usage: FM/AM Radio broadcasting, TV broadcasts, and Walkie-Talkies.

Microwaves

Features: Frequencies from 1 GHz to 300 GHz. They are strictly unidirectional (travel in a straight line). Sender and receiver antennas must be perfectly aligned (Line-of-Sight). Cannot penetrate heavy rain or thick walls easily.

Usage: Satellite communication, Radar, and Mobile tower backhaul links.

Infrared

Features: Extremely high frequencies used for short-range communication. It absolutely cannot penetrate walls (which is highly beneficial for security to prevent signal leaking).

Usage: TV remote controls, wireless keyboards, and short-range device sharing.

C. Special Features Analysis: Wired vs Wireless Connectivity 6-Mark PYQ

Features of Wired (Guided)

  • Immunity to Environmental Interference

    A buried fiber optic cable or a shielded Cat6 cable is largely unaffected by thunderstorms, heavy rain, or physical obstacles.

  • Consistent Reliability and Speed

    Provides dedicated, constant bandwidth. Serious gamers, server farms, and stock traders exclusively use wired Ethernet LANs because connections do not drop or fluctuate unexpectedly.

  • High Security

    To hack a wired network, a person must physically enter the building and tap the wire. It cannot be intercepted from the parking lot.

Features of Wireless (Unguided)

  • Ultimate Mobility (Greatest Feature)

    Users can walk around a campus with a smartphone or laptop while continuously streaming a high-definition video without losing connection.

  • Ease of Scalability & Deployment

    If an office hires 10 new employees, the IT admin just gives them the Wi-Fi password. In a wired network, engineers would have to physically run 10 new cables through the ceiling to their desks, which is costly and time-consuming.

  • Terrain Flexibility

    Essential for mountainous, highly forested, or rural areas where digging deep trenches to lay physical cables is impossible or economically too expensive.
