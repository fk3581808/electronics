PART 1: TRANSMISSION MEDIA

1. Magnetic Media

PYQ Focus (6 Marks)

List six devices that use magnetic media.

Before cables and wireless waves, the most common way to transfer massive amounts of data was physically writing it to magnetic media and transporting it via trucks or couriers (often jokingly called the "Sneakernet"). Even today, physically transporting a box of hard drives offers higher bandwidth (data per hour) than a slow internet connection.

List of 6 Devices that use Magnetic Media:

Hard Disk Drives (HDD) Used in desktop computers and servers.
Magnetic Tapes Used heavily in enterprise servers for mass data backup and archiving.
Floppy Disks Legacy removable storage.
Magnetic Stripe Cards The black stripe on the back of ATM / Credit cards and hotel key cards.
Audio Cassette Tapes Legacy audio recording media.
VCR / Videotapes (VHS) Legacy video recording media.

2. Twisted Pair Cable

PYQ Focus (6 Marks)

Explain the structural diagram of twisted pair cable and write its features. Compare coaxial cable and twisted pair cable.

A. Structural Diagram & Description

For your exam, draw this structure linearly from inside to outside.

1
Inner Copper Conductor:

The solid copper wire that actually carries the electrical data signal.

2
Color-Coded Plastic Insulation:

A plastic coating over the copper wire to prevent electrical short circuits.

3
The Twist:

Two insulated wires are twisted around each other in a helical (spiral) pattern.

4
Outer Protective Jacket:

A tough plastic/PVC outer sheath holding multiple twisted pairs together.

B. Why are they twisted? (Crucial Concept)

Twisting the wires cancels out Electromagnetic Interference (EMI) and Crosstalk from adjacent wires. If noise hits the cable, the twist ensures both wires are equally affected, allowing the receiver's differential amplifier to easily cancel the noise out.

C. Features of Twisted Pair Cable:

  • Cost: Cheapest and most widely used.
  • Usage: Ethernet LAN networks (Cat5e/Cat6) and old landlines.
  • Connectors: Uses RJ-45 (LAN) and RJ-11 (telephones).
  • Attenuation: High attenuation; signals degrade quickly, requiring repeaters every 100 meters.

3. Coaxial Cable

PYQ Focus

Explain the structural diagram of coaxial cable and write its features.

A. Structural Diagram & Description

Draw 4 concentric circular layers in your exam.

1
Inner Conductor (Solid Copper Core):

A stiff, solid copper wire in the dead center that carries the main electrical signal.

2
Dielectric Insulator:

A thick layer of solid or foam plastic surrounding the inner core to prevent it from touching the shield.

3
Metallic Braid / Shield:

A woven copper mesh or aluminum foil wrapped around the insulator. It acts as the second wire (ground) and provides massive protection against outside electromagnetic noise.

4
Outer Plastic Jacket:

A tough PVC covering to protect against physical damage and weather.

B. Features of Coaxial Cable:

  • High Bandwidth: Much higher bandwidth and data rate compared to Twisted Pair.
  • Noise Immunity: Excellent immunity against crosstalk and EMI because of the metallic shield.
  • Usage: Extensively used for Cable TV networks (Dish TV cables) and high-speed broadband internet.

C. Comparison: Coaxial Cable vs. Twisted Pair Cable PYQ

Feature Twisted Pair Cable Coaxial Cable
Structure Two insulated wires twisted together. Central core, insulator, and a metallic braided shield.
Bandwidth Lower bandwidth (Standard LAN speeds). Higher bandwidth (Can carry hundreds of TV channels simultaneously).
Noise Immunity Moderate. Vulnerable to high-level EMI. Excellent. The metal shield blocks external noise effectively.
Cost Very cheap and affordable. Relatively more expensive than twisted pair.
Installation Very thin, highly flexible, and easy to bend/install. Thicker, stiffer, and slightly harder to bend around tight corners.
Application Local Area Networks (Ethernet) and Telephones. Cable TV networks and long-distance analog transmission.

4. Power Lines (PLC)

Definition: Power Line Communication uses existing electrical power wires (the same wires that power your fans and lights) to simultaneously carry digital data.

Easy Word Explanation

You plug a special adapter into your normal wall socket. The internet signal rides on top of the 220V AC electrical current, turning your entire house's electrical wiring into a massive LAN cable.

Application Smart grids, remote meter reading, and HomePlug adapters (extending Wi-Fi to a dead zone using electrical sockets).

5. Fiber Optic Cable

PYQ Focus (6 Marks)

Explain the working principle of optical fiber with diagram + modes. Structural diagram + functions. State four advantages compared to copper.

Optical fibers use pulses of light to transmit data rather than electrical voltage.

A. Structural Diagram & Functions PYQ

Draw 3 to 4 cylindrical layers in your exam.

1
Core:

The ultra-thin, pure glass (or plastic) center where the light actually travels. Function: To carry the light signal.

2
Cladding:

Another layer of glass surrounding the core. Its Refractive Index is strictly less than the core. Function: To act like a mirror, reflecting light back into the core and preventing it from escaping.

3
Buffer Coating:

A plastic coating over the cladding. Function: To protect the fragile glass from moisture and physical shock.

4
Outer Jacket:

Made of Kevlar or tough PVC. Function: Protects the cable from being cut, crushed, or chewed by animals.

B. Working Principle PYQ

Optical fibers work on the principle of Total Internal Reflection (TIR).

  • When a light ray traveling in the denser Core hits the boundary of the rarer Cladding at an angle greater than the "Critical Angle", the light does not pass through. Instead, it acts like a mirror and reflects 100% of the light back into the core.
  • The light bounces zig-zag down the entire length of the cable at incredible speeds without leaking out.

C. Different Modes of Optical Fiber PYQ

Single-Mode Fiber (SMF)

Has an extremely thin core (approx. 9 microns). It only allows one single straight beam of laser light to pass through.

Used for: Very long distances (e.g., submarine cables under oceans) because there is no signal dispersion.

Multi-Mode Fiber (MMF)

Has a thicker core (50-62.5 microns). Multiple beams of light (usually from LEDs) bounce through at different angles simultaneously.

Used for: Shorter distances (e.g., within a university campus or data center).

D. Four Advantages Compared to Copper Cable PYQ

Infinite Bandwidth

Fiber can carry gigabits to terabits of data per second, far exceeding copper's limits.

Zero EMI

Since it uses light, it is 100% immune to electrical storms, high-voltage lines, and magnets.

Extremely Secure

You cannot easily tap or "listen in" on a fiber optic cable without breaking the glass, making it highly secure.

Lighter and Thinner

A single thin fiber optic cable can replace hundreds of heavy copper wires, saving massive space.

PART 2: TRANSMISSION CONTROL PROTOCOL (TCP)

PYQ Focus

Explain the working of the TCP. Role of addressing. Process of crash recovery.

1. What is a Protocol?

A Protocol is a standard set of rules and procedures that dictate how data is formatted, transmitted, and received across a computer network.

2. Working of TCP PYQ

TCP is the most important protocol of the Transport Layer. It provides reliable, ordered, and error-checked delivery of a stream of bytes between applications running on hosts communicating via an IP network.

Easy Word Explanation (The Registered Post)

If IP (Internet Protocol) is the postman who just drops the letter and runs away (unreliable), TCP is the registered speed-post service. TCP forces the receiver to sign a receipt (Acknowledgment). If the receipt isn't returned, TCP automatically prints a new letter and sends it again.

Features of TCP:

  • Connection-Oriented: Before sending any real data, TCP establishes a strict logical connection between sender and receiver.
  • Reliability: Guarantees 100% data delivery without loss or duplication.
  • Byte-Stream Service: Data is sent as a continuous stream of bytes, not just individual chunks.

3. Key Functions of TCP

PYQ Focus

A. Role of Addressing

Importance: While IP addresses identify the specific Computer/Device, TCP needs to know exactly which Application inside that computer should get the data (e.g., WhatsApp, Chrome, or Email?).

How TCP Addresses it: TCP uses Port Numbers (a 16-bit address).

Example: When data arrives at your laptop (IP Address), TCP checks the Port Number. If port=80, it goes to Web Browser. If port=25, it goes to Email. Without Port addressing, data delivery to the correct software is impossible.

B. Connection Management (3-Way Handshake)

TCP creates a connection using three steps:

  • 1. SYN: Client sends "Synchronize" packet -> "Can we talk?"
  • 2. SYN-ACK: Server replies -> "Yes, I acknowledge you, let's talk."
  • 3. ACK: Client replies -> "Great, acknowledging your yes. Sending data now."

C. Error & Flow Control

Error Control: TCP adds a mathematical Checksum. Receiver calculates it. If mismatch = discarded. TCP expects an ACK for every packet. If an ACK is lost, TCP retransmits.

Flow Control: Uses Sliding Window Protocol. Receiver says, "I only have 5MB space." Sender strictly sends 5MB and pauses, preventing receiver overwhelm.

D. Multiplexing

TCP allows multiple applications on the same computer to send data over a single IP network connection simultaneously by assigning different Port Numbers to each app.

E. Congestion Control

If network routers are jammed, TCP detects this (ACKs are delayed). It uses an algorithm called Slow Start, drastically slowing transmission speed, then slowly increasing it until stable.

F. Timer Management

TCP relies on timers. The most important is the Retransmission Timer (RTO). Sends a packet -> starts stopwatch. If no ACK arrives before zero, it assumes loss and resends.

4. Crash Recovery in TCP

PYQ Focus (6 Marks)

Describe the process of crash recovery.

Computers and routers can crash due to power failures or hardware faults. TCP must handle these crashes gracefully without breaking the entire network system.

Scenario 1: Receiving Host Crashes & Reboots

The Problem:

Sender A is sending data to Receiver B. Suddenly, B loses power, crashes, reboots, and loses all its memory of the active TCP connection.

The Recovery Process:
  1. Sender A does not know B crashed. A sends the next packet of data.
  2. Receiver B receives this packet but has no active connection for it in its memory.
  3. Receiver B immediately replies with a special TCP flag called RST (Reset).
  4. When Sender A receives the RST flag, it realizes B crashed. Sender A immediately aborts the old connection and tells the application to start a fresh connection from scratch.

Scenario 2: The Sender Host Crashes

The Problem:

Sender A crashes while Receiver B is patiently waiting for the next packet.

The Recovery Process:
  1. Receiver B waits. However, TCP utilizes a Keep-Alive Timer.
  2. If Receiver B hears nothing for a long time (e.g., 2 hours), the Keep-Alive timer expires.
  3. Receiver B sends "probe" packets asking "Are you still there?"
  4. If Sender A doesn't respond to the probes, Receiver B assumes A is dead and safely closes the connection, freeing up its memory resources.

Scenario 3: A Network Router Crashes

If an intermediate router crashes, the packets are lost. The sender's Retransmission Timer expires. The sender simply resends the packet. The IP network will automatically find a new, alternate router path to bypass the crashed router, and TCP will ensure the missing data is delivered successfully without the user ever noticing the crash.
