1. Wireless Transmission Media

PYQ Focus

Describe microwave transmission and its features/advantages. Differentiate between radio, microwave, and infrared transmission (4 points).

When we cannot use physical wires, we use unguided (wireless) transmission media to send electromagnetic waves through the air.

A. Radio Transmission

Definition: Radio waves are electromagnetic waves with frequencies ranging from 3 kHz to 1 GHz.

Features: They are omnidirectional, meaning they travel in all directions from the transmitting antenna. They can easily penetrate solid walls.

Electronics Example: FM/AM Radio broadcasts, Walkie-Talkies, and Maritime communication.
PYQ Focus

B. Microwave Transmission

Definition: Microwaves are higher frequency electromagnetic waves ranging from 1 GHz to 300 GHz.

Features: They are strictly unidirectional (Line-of-Sight). The sending and receiving antennas must be perfectly aligned facing each other.

Advantages:
  1. High bandwidth (can carry large amounts of data, like multiple video streams).
  2. Highly cost-effective for long distances over difficult terrain (mountains) where laying fiber optic cables is impossible.
Disadvantages:
  1. Rain Fade: High-frequency microwaves are easily absorbed by rain, snow, or fog, causing signal loss.
  2. Cannot penetrate solid obstacles (buildings, hills), requiring tall antenna towers.

Electronics Example: Satellite television (DTH), mobile network backhaul towers, and GPS.

C. Infra-red (IR)

Definition: High-frequency waves ranging from 300 GHz to 400 THz.

Features: Used strictly for short-range communication. They travel in a straight line and cannot penetrate solid objects like walls.

Ex: TV Remotes, Wireless mice, or old mobile data transfers.

D. Light (Li-Fi)

Definition: Uses visible or unguided laser light to transmit data. Requires strict line-of-sight and is blocked by physical objects.

Ex: Li-Fi (Light Fidelity) uses LED bulbs flickering at high speeds to transmit internet data inside a room.

E. Differentiate: Radio vs Microwave vs Infrared PYQ Table

Feature Radio Waves Microwaves Infrared
Frequency Range 3 kHz to 1 GHz 1 GHz to 300 GHz 300 GHz to 400 THz
Directionality Omnidirectional (travels everywhere) Unidirectional (Line of Sight) Highly directional (Line of Sight)
Wall Penetration Easily penetrates solid walls. Cannot penetrate walls easily. Blocked completely by any solid object.
Primary Usage FM/AM Broadcasting. Satellite links, Cellular towers. TV Remotes, short-range device sharing.

2. Wireless Communication Standards & Systems

PYQ Focus

Explain the IEEE standards for Bluetooth and Wi-Fi. Compare various wireless systems and explain which is best.

A. Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11)

  • Definition: Wi-Fi represents Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN) technologies. The standard governing it is IEEE 802.11.
  • Frequency: Operates in the 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz ISM (Industrial, Scientific, and Medical) radio bands.
  • Range & Speed: Typical range is 50-100 meters. Speeds range from 54 Mbps (802.11g) to over 9.6 Gbps (802.11ax / Wi-Fi 6).
Easy Explanation: It is the invisible internet bubble inside your house that lets your laptop browse the web without a LAN cable.

B. Bluetooth (IEEE 802.15.1)

  • Definition: Bluetooth is a standard for Wireless Personal Area Networks (WPAN). The standard governing it is IEEE 802.15.1.
  • Frequency: Also operates in the 2.4 GHz band but uses a technique called frequency-hopping spread spectrum to avoid interfering with Wi-Fi.
  • Range & Speed: Short range (typically up to 10 meters). Low speed (up to 3 Mbps).
  • Usage: Pairing wireless earphones to a smartphone or sending a small file to a nearby laptop.

C. Which Wireless System is the Best? PYQ

There is no single "best" system; it depends entirely on the application:

For Personal, short-range (0-10m):

Bluetooth is best due to low power consumption and easy pairing.

For Building/Campus (10-100m):

Wi-Fi is best because it provides high broadband speeds for multiple users simultaneously.

For Nationwide/Citywide (Long Range):

4G/5G Cellular Mobile Networks or Microwave systems are best because they offer extensive coverage and high-speed mobility.

3. Characterization of the Wireless Channel

PYQ Focus

Write the Characterization of the Wireless Channel. List any four characteristics of a wireless channel.

Unlike a physical copper wire which is stable and predictable, the "air" (wireless channel) is chaotic and unpredictable.

Characteristics of a Wireless Channel List of 4 for Exam

1. Path Loss (Attenuation)

As the radio signal travels away from the transmitter, its strength weakens (attenuates) over distance.

2. Multipath Fading

When a signal is transmitted, it bounces off buildings, mountains, and cars. These bounced signals arrive at the receiver at slightly different times, colliding with each other and causing the signal to distort or "fade".

Easy Ex: Imagine shouting in an empty, echoing hall. Your friend hears your original voice plus multiple echoes slightly delayed. It becomes confusing to understand.

3. Interference

The wireless channel is shared by everyone. Signals from other nearby networks (Co-channel interference) or different devices like microwaves (Adjacent-channel interference) can corrupt the data.

4. Doppler Shift

If the receiver (e.g., a person in a fast-moving train) is moving rapidly relative to the cell tower, the frequency of the received signal shifts, causing communication errors.

4. Receiver Techniques & Space-Time Coding

Because the wireless channel is chaotic (as seen above), the receiver must use special mathematical techniques to "clean up" the received signal.

1. Equalization

A mathematical filter at the receiver that attempts to reverse the distortion caused by multipath fading. It cancels out "Inter-Symbol Interference" (ISI) so 1s and 0s don't blur together.

2. Diversity Techniques

The receiver uses multiple antennas or frequencies to capture the signal. If one antenna gets a bad, faded signal, the other antenna might get a clear one.

3. Space-Time Coding (STC)

  • Definition: A technique used in MIMO (Multiple-Input Multiple-Output) systems (like modern Wi-Fi routers with 4 antennas).
  • How it works: It transmits multiple copies of the data stream across a number of antennas (Space) and across different time slots (Time).
Easy Word Explanation (The Courier Analogy)

If you want to make sure an urgent physical letter reaches your friend, you send the exact same letter through 3 different courier companies (Space) on 2 different days (Time). Even if 5 letters get lost, 1 will definitely survive. STC does this with data.

5. Mobility Management & Mobile IP

PYQ Focus

Explain mobility management. Explain mobile IP with a suitable diagram.

A. Mobility Management in Wireless Networks

Definition: Mobility management refers to the mechanisms used by cellular networks to track the physical location of a mobile subscriber and maintain their connection seamlessly as they move from one geographical area to another.

Importance/Functions:

Location Management (Roaming)

Tracking where your phone is located so the network knows which tower to send an incoming WhatsApp call to.

Handoff / Handover Management

When travelling in a bus, your phone moves out of range of Tower A to Tower B. Handoff ensures your ongoing call shifts from A to B without dropping.

B. Mobile IP (Internet Protocol)

Definition: Standard IP addresses are fixed to a location. If you move, your IP address changes, and your downloads will fail. Mobile IP is a protocol that allows a mobile node (like a laptop or smartphone) to keep the same continuous IP address even when it travels to different networks.

Key Components:

  • Mobile Node (MN) The smartphone or laptop.
  • Home Agent (HA) A router on your permanent home network.
  • Foreign Agent (FA) A router on the new network you are visiting.
  • Care-of Address (CoA): A temporary IP address given to you by the Foreign Agent.

6-Mark Diagram Description

Draw: Box 1 [Home Network] containing [Home Agent]. Box 2 [Foreign Network] containing [Foreign Agent] and [Mobile Node].
The Process:
1

The Mobile Node leaves its Home Network and connects to a Foreign Network (e.g., airport Wi-Fi).

2

The Foreign Agent gives the Mobile Node a temporary "Care-of Address".

3

The Mobile Node tells its Home Agent: "I am travelling. Here is my Care-of Address."

4

Now, if Google sends a packet to your device, it arrives at your Home Agent. The Home Agent intercepts the packet, puts it inside a new envelope (called Tunneling), and forwards it directly to your Foreign Agent, which delivers it to you.

Easy Word Explanation (The Hotel Analogy)

It’s like moving to a hotel in Delhi for a week. You tell your local Patna post office (Home Agent) your hotel address (Care-of Address). Whenever a letter arrives in Patna, the postmaster puts it in a bigger envelope and forwards it to your Delhi hotel (Foreign Agent).

6. Mobile Ad-Hoc Networks (MANETs) & Routing

PYQ Focus

Describe Ad-Hoc Networks Routing Protocols. Describe the performance of Dynamic Source Routing (DSR) and Cluster-Based Routing Protocol (CBRP).

A. What is a Mobile Ad-Hoc Network (MANET)?

A MANET is a temporary, decentralized wireless network. There is no central router, switch, or Wi-Fi hotspot. Instead, every single mobile device (node) acts as a router itself and forwards data for other devices.

Example: Military tanks in a desert connecting to each other directly because there are no cell towers.

B. Ad-Hoc Routing Protocols

Because devices are constantly moving, finding a route for data is very difficult. Protocols are divided into two types:

Proactive (Table-Driven)

Every node maintains a routing table of the whole network and updates it constantly, even if no data is being sent. (High battery waste).

Reactive (On-Demand)

Routes are only created when a node actually wants to send data. (Saves battery, but causes initial delay).

C. Performance Analysis of DSR and CBRP PYQ Focus

1. Dynamic Source Routing (DSR)

How it works: It is a reactive protocol. The sender (Source) determines the entire route path from source to destination and attaches this complete list of hops in the header of the data packet. Intermediate nodes just read the header and forward it.

Performance Analysis:
Pros: Excellent for networks with low mobility. Nodes don't need to waste battery updating tables constantly.
Cons: In highly dynamic networks with large data traffic, DSR performs poorly. Since the entire route is written inside the packet header, the header becomes very large, wasting bandwidth (Byte Overhead).

2. Cluster-Based Routing Protocol (CBRP)

How it works: Instead of every node trying to talk to every node, nodes are grouped into geographic "Clusters". One node is elected as the Cluster Head (CH). If Node A wants to talk to Node Z in a different cluster, Node A just gives the data to its CH, which routes it to Node Z's CH.

Performance Analysis:
Pros: Extremely scalable for massive networks. Reduces routing overhead significantly because only Cluster Heads perform complex routing tasks, saving battery of normal nodes.
Cons: If the Cluster Head moves away or its battery dies, the entire cluster breaks down temporarily until a new head is elected.

7. Cluster Techniques & Incremental Maintenance

A. Cluster Techniques in MANETs

Clustering divides a massive Ad-hoc network into smaller, manageable, overlapping sub-networks (clusters).

Nodes Roles:

Cluster Head

The leader of the cluster. Manages routing.

Cluster Gateway

Sits on the boundary of two clusters and acts as a bridge between them.

Cluster Member

Normal nodes that just send and receive their own data.

B. Incremental Cluster Maintenance Scheme

The Problem:

In a MANET, devices (nodes) are always moving (e.g., cars or drones). If a node moves out of a cluster, recalculating and forming the entire cluster system from scratch consumes massive CPU power and battery.

The Solution (Incremental Maintenance):

When the network topology changes (a node leaves or joins), we do not recalculate the entire network. Instead, the clustering algorithm only performs local, incremental updates.

Easy Word Explanation (The Classroom Analogy)

If one student leaves a class of 50, the principal doesn't dissolve the whole class and reassign everyone to new sections. They just update the attendance register to remove that one student locally. This saves massive amounts of time and energy (bandwidth).
