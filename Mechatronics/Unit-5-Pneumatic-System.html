1. Introduction to Pneumatic Systems

PYQ Focus

Describe pneumatic system. State the advantages, limitations and applications of pneumatic system. Discuss advantages of pneumatic systems over hydraulic systems. Explain the different components of a pneumatic system.

A. What is a Pneumatic System? PYQ

A Pneumatic System is a fluid power system that uses compressed air (or inert gases) to transmit force and motion. Unlike hydraulic systems that use liquid (oil), pneumatic systems suck in normal atmospheric air, compress it, and use that high-pressure air to push mechanical parts.

Easy Word Explanation (The Balloon Analogy)

Think of a balloon. When you blow air into it and let it go, the escaping air has the power to push the balloon forward. A pneumatic system harnesses this "push" of compressed air in a controlled way to do physical work.

Real-Life Examples: The automatic doors on a city bus (you can hear the "hiss" sound of air), the high-speed drill used by a dentist, and pneumatic nail guns used by carpenters.

B. Different Components of a Pneumatic System PYQ

Air Compressor

The machine that sucks in atmospheric air and compresses it to a high pressure.

Air Receiver / Tank

A large tank that stores the compressed air so the compressor doesn't have to run continuously.

FRL Unit (Air Treatment)

Cleans, regulates, and lubricates the air before it enters the delicate machine parts.

Direction Control Valves

Directs the flow of compressed air to the correct part of the actuator.

Pneumatic Actuator

Converts the compressed air energy into physical mechanical movement (Cylinders/Motors).

Exhaust

A port that vents the used air safely back into the atmosphere (causing the classic "hissing" sound).

C. Advantages and Limitations of Pneumatic Systems PYQ

Advantages

  • Availability of Fluid: Air is available everywhere for free and in infinite quantities.
  • Cleanliness: If a pipe leaks, it just blows air. No messy, dangerous oil spills. (Crucial for food/pharma).
  • Safety: Compressed air is non-explosive and non-flammable, making it safe in high fire-risk areas.
  • High Speed: Air is very light, allowing cylinders to move at extremely high speeds.
  • Simple Exhaust: Used air is simply dumped back into the room. No return pipes needed.

Limitations

  • Low Force: Because air is compressible (squishy), pneumatic systems cannot lift massively heavy loads like hydraulic systems can.
  • Noise: The exhaust of high-pressure air into the atmosphere is very loud.
  • Uneven Movement: Because air compresses and expands, the movement of the cylinder can sometimes be jerky, not smooth.

D. Advantages of Pneumatic over Hydraulic PYQ Comparison

Pneumatics is Cleaner: No messy oil leaks.

Pneumatics is Faster: Air travels through pipes faster than thick oil.

Pneumatics is Safer: No risk of hydraulic oil fires.

Pneumatics is Cheaper to build: Air is free, and the system doesn't require expensive heavy-duty return pipes.

2. Production, Distribution, & Conditioning

PYQ Focus

Stages of air treatment. Block diagram of three stages of preparation. Draw and explain the FRL unit. Purpose of a lubricator.

Compressed air straight from the atmosphere is dirty, wet, and hot. If sent directly to a machine, it will destroy the valves and cylinders. Therefore, it goes through Three Stages of Preparation.

A. Three Stages of Preparation of Compressed Air PYQ

Block Diagram (To draw in Exam):
[ Stage 1: Production (Compressor & Cooler) ] [ Stage 2: Distribution & Drying ] [ Stage 3: Conditioning (FRL Unit) ]

Stage 1: Production and Primary Treatment

  • Air Filter: Sucks in atmospheric air and removes large dust particles.
  • Compressor: Squashes the air to high pressure. This process makes the air extremely hot.
  • After-Cooler: Cools the hot compressed air down to room temperature. Cooling the air causes the invisible water vapor in it to turn into liquid water drops (condensation).

Stage 2: Distribution and Secondary Treatment

  • Moisture Separator: Removes the liquid water drops created by the cooler.
  • Air Receiver (Tank): Stores the air and allows remaining water to settle at the bottom.
  • Air Dryer: Uses chemicals or refrigeration to remove the last tiny bits of invisible moisture from the air. (Water causes internal rust, so it must be removed!)

Stage 3: Air Conditioning (The FRL Unit)

This happens right before the air enters the machine. (Explained in detail below).

B. The FRL Unit (Filter, Regulator, Lubricator) PYQ Focus

The FRL unit is the final, most crucial stage of air conditioning.

Diagram Description (For Exam):
  • Draw a pipe entering a box labeled Filter (draw a funnel shape inside to show dirt falling to a bottom bowl).
  • Connect it to a box labeled Regulator (draw a spring with an adjusting knob on top and a pressure gauge dial).
  • Connect it to a box labeled Lubricator (draw a small tube dropping a single drop of oil into the air stream).

Working Principle of FRL:

1. Filter: The air enters a spinning bowl. Centrifugal force throws the remaining fine dust and tiny water droplets to the walls, where they fall to the bottom and are drained out.
2. Regulator: Air pressure from the compressor fluctuates. The regulator uses a spring and diaphragm to ensure the air coming out is at a constant, steady, smooth pressure (e.g., strictly 5 bar), protecting delicate valves from bursting.
3. Lubricator (Crucial PYQ): Compressed air is completely dry and will cause metal valves and cylinders to scrape against each other, causing extreme friction and wear. The Lubricator injects a microscopic mist of lubricating oil into the air stream. The air carries this oil mist into the machine, continuously oiling the moving parts from the inside.
Exam Answer for "Purpose of Lubricator":

To reduce internal friction, prevent rust, minimize wear and tear of sliding seals, and increase the lifespan of pneumatic actuators and valves by providing automatic internal oiling via the air stream.

3. Pneumatic Actuators

PYQ Focus

What do you mean by pneumatic actuator? Classify actuators. Describe single acting cylinder with neat diagram. Explain end position cushioning.

A. What is a Pneumatic Actuator? PYQ

A Pneumatic Actuator is the "muscle" of the system. It is a mechanical device that converts the potential energy of compressed air into kinetic energy (physical mechanical motion and force).

B. Classification of Actuators PYQ

  • Linear Actuators (Cylinders): Produce straight-line push/pull motion. (Single-Acting & Double-Acting).
  • Rotary Actuators (Air Motors): Produce continuous spinning motion (like a pneumatic drill).
  • Limited Angle Actuators: Produce a twisting motion up to a certain angle (e.g., 90° or 180°).

C. Single Acting Cylinder PYQ

Diagram Description:
  • Draw a horizontal rectangular tube (the cylinder barrel).
  • Inside, draw a vertical block (the Piston) with a long horizontal rod (the Piston Rod) coming out of the right side.
  • On the left side of the cylinder, draw a small hole/pipe for "Air IN".
  • On the right side, inside the barrel, draw a coiled spring pushing against the piston.
Extension:

When high-pressure air enters the left port, it pushes the piston to the right, compressing the spring. The rod extends and pushes a load.

Retraction:

When the air supply is turned off and the left port is opened to the atmosphere (exhaust), the trapped compressed air escapes. The mechanical spring automatically pushes the piston back to its original starting position.

Application: Clamping a piece of wood on a table, or punching a hole in paper.

D. End Position Cushioning PYQ Focus

The Problem:

In a double-acting cylinder moving at very high speeds, the heavy metal piston slams violently into the metal end-caps of the cylinder at the end of its stroke. This causes massive noise, vibration, and destroys the cylinder over time.

The Solution & How it works:

It is a built-in shock absorber.

Just before the piston reaches the very end of its stroke, a small plunger on the piston blocks the main exhaust port. The escaping air is trapped in a small pocket. This trapped air acts like an "air spring" or a cushion. It can only escape through a tiny, adjustable needle valve.

Result: This smoothly and gently slows the piston down in the last few millimeters of its journey, ensuring a soft, quiet stop instead of a violent metal-on-metal crash.

4. System Components & Graphic Representations (Symbols)

PYQ Focus

Explain the standard graphical symbols used in pneumatic circuits.

In mechatronics, engineers do not draw realistic pictures of compressors and valves. They use standard ISO graphic symbols to design circuits. (Note for exam: Describe these shapes accurately or sketch them).

Pneumatic Compressor

A circle with a hollow (unfilled) triangle pointing upwards. (Note: A filled/black triangle means hydraulic oil; hollow means air).

Pneumatic Exhaust

A small triangle pointing downwards (showing air escaping to the atmosphere).

Air Receiver (Tank)

A capsule shape (a rectangle with semi-circles on the left and right ends).

FRL Unit (Simplified)

A diamond shape. Inside: vertical dashed line (Filter), straight horizontal line on top (Lubricator), and a line crossing it with a spring symbol (Regulator).

Single-Acting Cylinder

Rectangle containing a T-shaped piston/rod. A zig-zag line (spring) is drawn inside the front. Only one connection line enters the back.

Double-Acting Cylinder

Rectangle with a T-shaped piston and rod. NO spring. Two connection lines: one at the back and one at the front.

5. Design of Systems (Basic Pneumatic Circuit)

To tie it all together, here is how a basic pneumatic circuit is designed to operate a Single-Acting Cylinder.

The Circuit Flow:

1

The Compressor generates air and stores it in the Receiver.

2

The air flows through the FRL Unit, becoming clean, pressure-regulated, and lubricated.

3

The air waits at the 'P' (Pressure) port of a 3/2 Push-Button Direction Control Valve.

4

When the human operator presses the button, the valve shifts. Air travels from 'P' to 'A' (Actuator port).

5

The air enters the Single-Acting Cylinder, pushing the piston out to stamp a box.

6

When the operator releases the button, a spring inside the valve shifts it back. The air inside the cylinder escapes to the atmosphere via the Exhaust port, and the cylinder's internal spring retracts the piston.
